October 2025 — Sweden & Nordic business briefing

Executive summary
Sweden’s macro picture in late September–October is now defined by accelerating disinflation and a Riksbank that signals patience with current interest-rate settings. Parallel structural themes — payments modernisation (including the e‑krona), financial‑system resilience and continued energy‑sector capital allocation shifts — present near‑term operational priorities and mid‑term strategic opportunities for Nordic corporates.

Main developments (high‑impact items, short summaries and strategic implications)

- Disinflation accelerates: headline CPI 0.9% in September 2025
  Summary: Preliminary data show Sweden’s annual CPI eased to 0.9% in September (down from 1.1% in August), slightly below market expectations and reinforcing a clear downward inflation trend [2].
  Why it matters: Lower inflation reduces short‑term input‑cost pressure but can mask weaker demand and compress nominal revenue growth. Pricing power and top‑line planning should be revisited.
  Strategic actions: Reassess pricing escalation clauses, run revenue scenarios with lower nominal growth, and prioritise margin improvements via productivity and cost rationalisation [2].

- Riksbank: confidence in current rate path, inclination to hold
  Summary: Minutes from the Riksbank’s September meeting indicate officials are confident that the current policy rate will guide inflation back to target and that further tightening is not currently necessary; the Monetary Policy Report provides the updated forecast envelope and scenario analysis supporting this view [1][3].
  Why it matters: Stability in policy rates reduces immediate refinancing shock risk but leaves borrowing costs elevated relative to pre‑pandemic norms.
  Strategic actions: Treasury teams should selectively lock maturities where advantageous, stress‑test debt service under slower revenue scenarios, and align forecast windows with Riksbank publication dates to improve timing on funding decisions [1][3][8].

- Payments transformation & resilience: Payments Report 2025
  Summary: The Riksbank’s Payments Report 2025 documents rapid electronic payment adoption, market consolidation among PSPs, and an elevated focus on operational resilience and access to core payment infrastructure [6].
  Why it matters: Payments are both a cost and a data asset; failures or friction create direct revenue loss and reputational damage.
  Strategic actions: Accelerate omni‑channel payment integration, invest in fraud prevention and outage recovery, and evaluate partnerships (or M&A) to capture transaction data value and defend against fintech disintermediation [6].

- E‑krona work moves from concept toward operational readiness
  Summary: The Riksbank continues its e‑krona programme and is actively analysing design, legal and technical implications — with increased emphasis on international coordination (digital euro developments) and offline/Climate impact considerations [1][5][12].
  Why it matters: A central‑bank digital currency would change settlement flows, liquidity management, KYC requirements and the competitive landscape for wallets and payment services.
  Strategic actions: Establish cross‑functional e‑krona readiness teams (IT, legal, payments, treasury), run vendor and standards assessments, and pilot wallet/settlement concepts to capture first‑mover advantages and reduce future migration costs [1][12].

- Financial‑stability and operational resilience remain front‑of‑mind
  Summary: Riksbank materials and communications increasingly tie monetary policy judgement to financial‑stability considerations and payment system resilience; the public calendar and statistics give firms forward visibility on data releases and decisions [6][8][11].
  Why it matters: Policy and market moves can be abrupt when stability risks crystallise; transparency helps firms prepare but also demands disciplined scenario planning.
  Strategic actions: Elevate liquidity stress‑testing, run contingency playbooks for payment outages and settlement frictions, and synchronise board reporting with the Riksbank calendar to ensure timely decisions [6][8][11].

- Energy sector: Q3 momentum and capital allocation shifts
  Summary: Energy and related stocks performed strongly in Q3 2025, and major regional players are adjusting capital allocation (buybacks, dividend posture, targeted capex) in response to commodity cycles and decarbonisation needs [9][10].
  Why it matters: Energy price dynamics and capital allocation choices influence supplier costs, project finance availability and strategic partnerships across industrial sectors.
  Strategic actions: Revisit capex timing and optionality for electrification projects, consider opportunistic capital‑return strategies if balance sheets permit, and stress‑test supply‑chain exposure to energy price swings [9][10].

- Labour market: pockets of wage pressure despite disinflation
  Summary: Falling headline inflation provides negotiating room for broader wage moderation, but structural skill shortages (tech, engineering, healthcare) can sustain localized wage inflation — a factor the Riksbank emphasises as a key inflation driver to watch [1][3].
  Why it matters: Heterogenous wage dynamics mean blanket compensation freezes risk talent loss in scarce roles.
  Strategic actions: Implement differentiated pay strategies: protect critical skills, expand reskilling/internal mobility, and link general increases to productivity and measurable performance.

- Exporters & FX: relative inflation differentials matter
  Summary: Continued disinflation can support real exchange competitiveness over time, but outcomes depend on relative inflation and rate moves across trading partners; the Riksbank’s FX statistics and calendar are practical inputs for scenario work [11][9].
  Strategic actions: Update FX hedge policies and pricing clauses, run competitor‑benchmark scenarios, and align export contracts with macro publication cycles.

Practical near‑term priorities (90‑day checklist for leaders)
- Run a liquidity/debt‑service stress test using Riksbank forecast bands [1][3].
- Launch an e‑krona readiness sprint (vendor map, pilot concepts, legal gap analysis) [1][12].
- Execute a payments resilience audit (end‑to‑end testing, fraud controls, backup rails) [6].
- Review compensation frameworks with segmented plans for scarce roles and productivity levers [1][3].
